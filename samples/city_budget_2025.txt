CITY OF MAPLETON — FISCAL YEAR 2025 ADOPTED OPERATING BUDGET
Public Document — Published by the Office of Management and Budget
Effective Date: July 1, 2024

1. OVERVIEW
The City of Mapleton's total adopted operating budget for Fiscal Year 2025 is
$48.2 million, an increase of 6.4% over the FY2024 adopted budget of $45.3
million. The budget is fully funded by property taxes (52%), state and federal
intergovernmental aid (27%), fees and permits (14%), and miscellaneous revenue
(7%). The City maintains a reserve fund balance of $7.1 million, equal to
approximately 14.7% of annual operating expenditures.

2. GENERAL FUND APPROPRIATIONS BY DEPARTMENT
The General Fund accounts for $41.6 million of the total budget. Departmental
appropriations for FY2025 are:
- Public Safety (Police and Fire): $18.9 million (45.4% of the General Fund)
- Public Works and Sanitation: $7.4 million
- Parks, Recreation, and Cultural Affairs: $4.2 million
- Health and Human Services: $3.8 million
- Administration and Finance: $3.1 million
- Planning and Community Development: $2.6 million
- Debt Service: $1.6 million

3. CAPITAL IMPROVEMENT PROGRAM
The FY2025 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) totals $6.6 million, funded
through a combination of general obligation bonds ($4.0 million), the capital
reserve ($1.8 million), and grants ($0.8 million). Major projects include the
Riverside Bridge rehabilitation ($2.3 million), water main replacement on
Elm Street ($1.4 million), and the Central Library HVAC modernization
($0.9 million).

4. STAFFING AND PERSONNEL
The adopted budget authorizes 412 full-time equivalent (FTE) positions, a net
increase of 9 FTEs from FY2024. New positions include six firefighters funded
under a federal SAFER grant, two sanitation workers, and one sustainability
coordinator. Total personnel costs, including salaries and benefits, account
for 61% of the General Fund.

5. REVENUE ASSUMPTIONS AND RISKS
Property tax revenue assumes a 2.1% growth in assessed valuation and a
collection rate of 98.5%. Intergovernmental aid is held flat year-over-year;
the budget notes that any reduction in state aid would create a mid-year
funding gap. The budget does not assume any new tax rate increase. Sales-tax-
adjacent permit revenue is projected to decline 3% due to slower commercial
construction.

6. NOTABLE YEAR-OVER-YEAR CHANGES
Public Safety appropriations rose $1.1 million (6.2%) over FY2024, driven by
contractual wage increases and the six SAFER-grant firefighters. Parks and
Recreation increased $0.4 million to fund expanded summer youth programming.
The sustainability coordinator is a newly created role tied to the City's 2030
Climate Action Plan. Debt service decreased $0.2 million as the 2009 series
bonds reached maturity.
